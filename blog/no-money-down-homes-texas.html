How to Buy a House With No Money Down in Texas (2026 Guide)

üìÖ February 14, 2026 üë§ Stromation Homes Team ‚è± 10 min read

The biggest barrier to homeownership is almost always the same thing: the down payment. Scraping together $40,000 or $60,000 for a 20% down payment on a Dallas-area home feels impossible for many buyers, especially with rising rents eating into savings. But here is the truth that most people do not realize: you do not actually need a large down payment to buy a house in Texas. In some cases, you can buy with literally zero dollars down.

This guide covers every legitimate path to buying a home with no money down in the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex, including VA loans, USDA loans, down payment assistance programs, and creative strategies that can get you the keys without draining your savings account.

VA Loans: The Gold Standard for Zero Down Payment

If you are a veteran, active-duty service member, or eligible surviving spouse, a VA loan is the single best mortgage product available in the United States. Backed by the Department of Veterans Affairs, VA loans offer 0% down payment, no private mortgage insurance (PMI), and typically the lowest interest rates on the market.

VA Loan Eligibility Requirements

  • Active-duty service members: Currently serving with at least 90 continuous days of service
  • Veterans: Served at least 90 days during wartime or 181 days during peacetime
  • National Guard/Reserves: At least 6 years of service, or 90 days of active-duty deployment
  • Surviving spouses: Un-remarried spouse of a veteran who died in service or from a service-connected disability

To get started, you will need a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) from the VA, which your lender can often pull electronically in minutes. Most VA lenders look for a minimum credit score of 620, though the VA itself sets no minimum.

VA Loan Limits in DFW (2026)

As of 2026, VA loans have no loan limit for borrowers with full entitlement. That means if you have never used your VA benefit before (or have fully restored it), you can borrow as much as a lender will approve with zero down. For DFW, where the median home price sits around $350,000-$400,000, this makes VA loans extremely powerful. Even homes in higher-priced areas like Plano or McKinney are well within reach with a VA loan.

üí° VA Loan Cost Advantage

On a $350,000 home, a conventional buyer putting 5% down would pay approximately $165/month in PMI plus their $17,500 down payment. A VA buyer pays $0 down and $0 in PMI, saving over $77,000 over the life of the loan. The VA funding fee (2.15% for first-time use) can be rolled into the loan amount.

USDA Loans: Zero Down for Rural Areas Near DFW

USDA Rural Development loans are another true zero-down-payment option, and you do not need to be a veteran to qualify. These loans are designed for moderate-income buyers purchasing homes in USDA-eligible rural areas. The surprising part? Several growing communities on the outskirts of DFW still qualify.

DFW-Area Communities That May Qualify for USDA Loans

  • Aubrey: A fast-growing community north of Denton with new construction and a small-town feel
  • Celina: One of the fastest-growing cities in Texas, with many areas still USDA-eligible
  • Parts of Prosper: Outer sections of this booming suburb may still fall within USDA boundaries
  • Anna and Princeton: Communities northeast of McKinney with affordable new construction
  • Weston and Melissa: Small towns along the 75 corridor with eligible properties

USDA eligibility is based on the specific property address, not just the city. Boundaries change as areas grow, so always verify a property's eligibility on the USDA eligibility map at rd.usda.gov before making an offer.

USDA Loan Requirements

  • Income limits: Household income cannot exceed 115% of the area median income (roughly $110,000-$120,000 for a family of four in DFW)
  • Credit score: Most lenders require 640 or higher
  • Primary residence: Must be your primary home, not an investment property
  • Property location: Must fall within a USDA-eligible area

USDA loans do charge a guarantee fee (1% upfront, 0.35% annually), but this is significantly less than PMI on a conventional loan, and the upfront fee can be financed into the loan.

Down Payment Assistance Programs That Cover 100%

Even if you do not qualify for VA or USDA loans, Texas has some of the most generous down payment assistance (DPA) programs in the country. These programs can cover your entire down payment and sometimes closing costs too, effectively turning an FHA or conventional loan into a zero-down purchase.

TSAHC (Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation)

TSAHC offers two programs that are available to all Texas buyers, not just first-time purchasers:

  • Homes for Texas Heroes: For teachers, firefighters, EMS workers, police officers, corrections officers, and veterans. Offers up to 5% of the loan amount as a grant (free money) or a forgivable second lien.
  • Home Sweet Texas: For all eligible buyers. Provides up to 5% in down payment and closing cost assistance as a grant or deferred forgivable second lien.

On a $300,000 home, that is up to $15,000 in free assistance. Combined with an FHA loan requiring only 3.5% down ($10,500), the TSAHC grant more than covers the down payment, leaving you with extra for closing costs.

My First Texas Home Program

Administered by the Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs, My First Texas Home provides down payment assistance up to 5% of the mortgage amount, paired with a competitive 30-year fixed-rate mortgage. Despite the name, you do not need to be a first-time buyer if you are purchasing in a targeted area, and much of Denton County and parts of Dallas County qualify as targeted areas.

üí° Stack Your Benefits

Some buyers combine a TSAHC grant with seller concessions to cover both the down payment and closing costs. On a $320,000 FHA purchase, TSAHC covers the $11,200 down payment, and the seller contributes up to 6% ($19,200) toward closing costs. Your total out-of-pocket at closing could be close to $0.

Seller Concessions: Getting the Seller to Pay Your Way In

Seller concessions are contributions from the home seller toward your closing costs. While they do not technically reduce your down payment, they reduce the total cash you need at the closing table, which has the same practical effect.

How Much Can Sellers Contribute?

  • FHA loans: Sellers can contribute up to 6% of the purchase price
  • VA loans: Sellers can contribute up to 4% plus all normal closing costs
  • Conventional loans (less than 10% down): Sellers can contribute up to 3%
  • USDA loans: Sellers can contribute up to 6% of the purchase price

In the current DFW market, seller concessions are negotiable. Homes that have been sitting on the market for 30 or more days, properties needing repairs, or sellers motivated by a job relocation are the best candidates for concession requests. Your agent can advise you on when and how much to ask for.

Gift Funds: Using Family Money for Your Down Payment

If a family member wants to help you buy a home, gift funds are a legitimate and commonly used strategy. Most loan programs allow gift funds to cover part or all of your down payment, but there are specific rules you need to follow.

Gift Fund Rules by Loan Type

  • FHA loans: 100% of the down payment can come from gifts. Donors must be family members, employers, or qualified organizations.
  • Conventional loans: Gifts are allowed, but if you put less than 20% down, at least 5% may need to come from your own funds (varies by lender).
  • VA loans: Gift funds are allowed for closing costs since there is no down payment required.

You will need a signed gift letter stating the money is a gift (not a loan), the donor's bank statements showing the source of funds, and documentation of the transfer. Your lender will provide the exact gift letter format they require.

Lease-to-Own: A Longer Path to Zero Down

Lease-to-own (also called rent-to-own) agreements let you rent a home with the option to purchase it later, usually within 1-3 years. A portion of your monthly rent payments is credited toward the eventual purchase price or down payment.

This option works best for buyers who need time to improve their credit score, build savings, or establish employment history. However, approach lease-to-own arrangements with caution:

  • Get an attorney involved: These contracts are complex and Texas law does not heavily regulate them
  • Verify the seller owns the home free and clear or is current on their mortgage
  • Understand what happens if you choose not to buy: You may forfeit your option fee and rent credits
  • Get an inspection before signing: Treat it like a real purchase from day one

Realistic Expectations and Important Caveats

While buying a home with no money down is absolutely possible, you should go in with your eyes open. Here are the realities every zero-down buyer should understand:

  • You will start with zero equity: If home values dip even slightly, you could owe more than your home is worth. In DFW's strong market this risk is lower, but it exists.
  • Your monthly payment will be higher: Borrowing more means higher monthly mortgage payments. Make sure your budget has room for this.
  • You still need cash for other costs: Home inspections ($350-$500), the option period fee ($100-$500), appraisal fees ($450-$600), and moving expenses all require cash upfront. Budget at least $2,000-$3,000 for these costs.
  • Not all homes qualify: USDA and some DPA programs have property requirements. The home must meet minimum condition standards and fall within eligible areas.
  • Programs have income and purchase price limits: Make sure you check the current limits before falling in love with a home that is too expensive for the program you are using.

üí° The Bottom Line

Zero down does not mean zero cost. But it does mean you can become a homeowner in DFW years sooner than you thought possible. The key is working with a lender who knows Texas assistance programs and an agent who understands which properties and areas qualify for each program.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I really buy a house with zero down payment in Texas? +

Yes, it is possible through several legitimate pathways. VA loans offer true 0% down payment for eligible veterans and active-duty military. USDA loans offer 0% down for homes in eligible rural areas near DFW, including communities like Aubrey, Celina, and parts of Prosper. Additionally, Texas down payment assistance programs like TSAHC and My First Texas Home can cover your entire down payment on FHA or conventional loans, effectively making them zero-down purchases. Each program has its own eligibility requirements, so work with a knowledgeable lender to find the right fit.

What credit score do I need for a no-money-down home loan in Texas? +

Credit score requirements vary by program. For VA loans, most lenders require a minimum credit score of 620, though the VA itself does not set a minimum. USDA loans typically require a 640 credit score for their automated approval system. Down payment assistance programs vary, but most require at least 620-660. FHA loans (which can be paired with DPA grants to become effectively zero-down) accept scores as low as 580. Higher credit scores will always get you better interest rates, so it pays to improve your score before applying.

Which DFW suburbs qualify for USDA zero-down loans? +

USDA eligibility maps change periodically as areas grow, but as of 2026, communities on the outer edges of the DFW metroplex may qualify. These include Aubrey, Celina, parts of Prosper, Anna, Princeton, Weston, Melissa, and other areas. Eligibility is determined by the specific property address, not just the city name. Some homes within a qualifying city may be eligible while others are not. Always use the USDA eligibility map tool at rd.usda.gov to check a specific address before making assumptions or offers.

Are there income limits for no-down-payment programs in Texas? +

It depends on the program. VA loans have no income limits at all, making them the most flexible option. USDA loans cap household income at 115% of the area median income, which in DFW is approximately $110,000-$120,000 for a family of four. TSAHC programs have income limits that vary by county and household size but are generally generous enough to include moderate-income buyers earning up to $100,000 or more. My First Texas Home also has income limits that vary by county. Check each program's current limits on their official websites or ask your lender for the latest figures.
