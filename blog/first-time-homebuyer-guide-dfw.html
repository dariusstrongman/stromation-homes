The Complete First-Time Home Buyer's Guide to DFW (2026)

üìÖ February 13, 2026 üë§ Stromation Homes Team ‚è± 9 min read

Buying your first home in the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex is an exciting milestone, but it can also feel overwhelming. With one of the fastest-growing housing markets in the country, understanding the process and your options is crucial to making a smart investment.

This comprehensive guide walks you through every step of buying your first home in DFW, from getting pre-approved to closing day and beyond. Whether you're looking in bustling Dallas neighborhoods, family-friendly suburbs like Frisco and Plano, or the expanding communities of Fort Worth, this guide has you covered.

Step 1: Get Pre-Approved for a Mortgage

Before you start scrolling through home listings, getting pre-approved for a mortgage is essential. Pre-approval tells you exactly how much home you can afford and shows sellers you're a serious buyer in DFW's competitive market.

What You'll Need for Pre-Approval

  • Proof of income: Recent pay stubs (last 2 months), W-2s (last 2 years), and tax returns
  • Employment verification: Letter from employer or recent bank statements showing direct deposits
  • Credit report: Lenders will pull this, but check yours first at AnnualCreditReport.com
  • Asset documentation: Bank statements, investment accounts, retirement accounts
  • Debt information: Student loans, car payments, credit card balances
  • Identification: Driver's license and Social Security number

In DFW, most lenders can pre-approve you within 24-48 hours. Shop around with at least three lenders to compare interest rates and terms. Even a 0.25% difference in interest rates can save you tens of thousands over a 30-year mortgage.

üí° DFW First-Time Buyer Tip

Texas has no state income tax, which means your take-home pay goes further. However, property taxes in DFW counties average 2-2.5% of home value annually, higher than the national average. Factor this into your budget when calculating affordability.

Step 2: Understanding Down Payment Options

One of the biggest myths about home buying is that you need 20% down. While putting down 20% helps you avoid Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) and get better rates, many first-time buyers in Texas successfully purchase homes with much less.

Common Down Payment Scenarios

  • Conventional loans (3-5% down): Available through most lenders, ideal if you have good credit (680+)
  • FHA loans (3.5% down): Government-backed, accept lower credit scores (580+), popular in DFW
  • VA loans (0% down): For veterans and active military, no PMI required, excellent rates
  • USDA loans (0% down): For homes in eligible rural areas (some DFW suburbs qualify)

Texas Down Payment Assistance Programs

Texas offers several programs specifically designed to help first-time buyers:

My First Texas Home (MFTH): Offers down payment assistance up to 5% of the loan amount, plus competitive interest rates. Available through approved lenders statewide. Income and purchase price limits apply.

Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC): Provides up to $15,000 in down payment assistance grants (not loans) for eligible buyers. Available in most DFW counties.

Local Programs: Many DFW cities offer additional assistance:

  • City of Dallas: First Time Homebuyer Program (up to $10,000)
  • Fort Worth HOME Program: Offers down payment and closing cost assistance
  • Plano Housing Authority: Various first-time buyer assistance options
  • Frisco Affordable Housing: Down payment assistance for eligible areas

Step 3: Choosing the Right DFW Neighborhood

The Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex spans over 9,000 square miles with dozens of distinct cities and neighborhoods. Where you buy depends on your priorities: schools, commute, lifestyle, and budget.

Popular First-Time Buyer Areas in DFW

North Dallas Suburbs (Plano, Frisco, McKinney, Allen): Excellent schools, master-planned communities, newer construction. Median home prices: $350,000-$500,000. Great for families prioritizing education and suburban amenities.

Mid-Cities (Irving, Grand Prairie, Euless, Bedford): Convenient location between Dallas and Fort Worth, diverse communities, more affordable. Median home prices: $250,000-$350,000. Ideal for first-time buyers balancing price and location.

Fort Worth Suburbs (Keller, Southlake, Flower Mound): Top-rated schools, lower density, upscale communities. Median home prices: $400,000-$600,000. Worth the premium if school quality is your top priority.

East Dallas Neighborhoods (Lake Highlands, Casa View, Lakewood): Walkable, established neighborhoods, character homes. Median home prices: $300,000-$450,000. Popular with young professionals wanting urban amenities.

Southern Suburbs (Cedar Hill, Mansfield, Desoto): Growing communities, newer developments, more affordable. Median home prices: $250,000-$350,000. Great value for buyers on a tighter budget.

Key Factors to Consider

  • Commute time: DFW traffic can be intense. Use Google Maps during rush hour to test your potential commute.
  • School districts: Even if you don't have kids, good schools boost resale value. Check GreatSchools.org ratings.
  • Property taxes: Vary significantly by city and school district (1.8% to 2.7% of home value).
  • HOA fees: Many newer communities have HOAs ranging from $50-$500/month.
  • Future development: Research planned infrastructure projects that could impact property values.

Step 4: Working with a Real Estate Agent

In Texas, buyer's agents are typically paid by the seller (not you), making professional representation essentially free for buyers. A knowledgeable DFW agent provides invaluable benefits:

  • Access to new listings before they hit public sites
  • Expertise in specific neighborhoods and school districts
  • Negotiation skills to get the best price and terms
  • Guidance through inspection, appraisal, and closing
  • Connections to lenders, inspectors, and contractors

Look for an agent who specializes in first-time buyers and knows your target neighborhoods well. Ask for references and check online reviews. The right agent can save you thousands and help you avoid costly mistakes.

Step 5: Making an Offer and Negotiating

When you find "the one," your agent will help you craft a competitive offer. In DFW's hot market, expect competition, especially in desirable areas under $400,000.

Components of Your Offer

  • Purchase price: Based on comparable sales and market conditions
  • Earnest money: Typically 1-2% of purchase price, held in escrow
  • Option period: In Texas, buyers pay $100-$500 for 7-10 days to inspect and negotiate repairs
  • Financing contingency: Protects you if your loan falls through
  • Closing date: Usually 30-45 days from offer acceptance
  • Requested repairs or credits: Based on home condition

In competitive situations, consider offering above asking price, limiting repair requests, or writing a personal letter to the sellers. Your agent will guide you on the best strategy for your situation.

Step 6: Home Inspection and Due Diligence

During your option period, hire a professional home inspector ($300-$500 in DFW) to thoroughly examine the property. Texas law doesn't require specific disclosures, making inspections even more critical.

What Inspectors Examine

  • Foundation (crucial in North Texas clay soil)
  • Roof condition and age
  • HVAC systems (summer cooling is expensive in Texas)
  • Plumbing and water pressure
  • Electrical systems and panel capacity
  • Attic insulation and ventilation
  • Windows, doors, and weatherstripping

After the inspection, you can negotiate repairs, request credits, or walk away if issues are too severe. Don't skip this step, even on newer homes. We've seen brand-new construction with significant defects that needed addressing before closing.

Step 7: Understanding Closing Costs

Beyond your down payment, expect to pay 2-5% of the purchase price in closing costs. On a $300,000 home, that's $6,000-$15,000.

Typical Buyer Closing Costs in Texas

  • Lender fees: Origination, underwriting, processing ($1,500-$3,000)
  • Title insurance: Owner's policy (0.9% of purchase price)
  • Appraisal: Required by lender ($450-$600)
  • Home inspection: Paid during option period ($300-$500)
  • Survey: Property boundary verification ($400-$600)
  • Recording fees: County filing fees ($200-$400)
  • Prepaid items: Property taxes, insurance, HOA dues (varies)
  • Escrow setup: Initial deposit for tax and insurance escrow ($500-$2,000)

You can often negotiate for the seller to contribute toward closing costs, especially if you're paying full price or above. Your lender will provide a Loan Estimate within three days of application showing exact costs.

Step 8: Final Walkthrough and Closing Day

The day before closing, do a final walkthrough to ensure the home is in the agreed-upon condition and that any negotiated repairs were completed. Check that all appliances included in the sale are still there and functioning.

What to Bring to Closing

  • Government-issued photo ID
  • Certified check or proof of wire transfer for closing costs
  • Proof of homeowner's insurance
  • Final walkthrough notes (if any issues arose)

Closing typically takes 1-2 hours at a title company. You'll sign dozens of documents, including the promissory note, deed of trust, and closing disclosure. Read everything carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear. Once you sign, you'll receive the keys to your new home.

üéâ Congratulations, You're a Homeowner!

After closing, set up utilities, change the locks, and start making your new DFW house a home. Keep all closing documents in a safe place and set up your mortgage auto-payment to never miss a due date.

Common First-Time Buyer Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping pre-approval: You'll miss out on homes in competitive markets
  • Maxing out your budget: Leave room for repairs, furniture, and emergencies
  • Ignoring property taxes: Texas property taxes are high; factor them into monthly costs
  • Waiving inspection: Never skip this, even in bidding wars
  • Forgetting about HOA fees: These add to your monthly housing costs
  • Making big purchases before closing: Don't buy a car or furniture on credit before your loan closes
  • Not researching the neighborhood: Visit at different times of day and week
  • Falling in love with the first home: See at least 10-15 homes before deciding

Why Now is a Great Time to Buy in DFW

Despite market fluctuations, DFW remains one of the strongest real estate markets in the country. Major corporations continue relocating here, bringing high-paying jobs. Population growth shows no signs of slowing. While home prices have appreciated significantly, so have wages and rental rates.

For first-time buyers, waiting often means paying more later. If you have stable income, good credit, and plan to stay in DFW for at least 3-5 years, buying now can build wealth and provide housing stability.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do I need for a down payment in Texas? +

While conventional loans often require 20% down, first-time buyers in Texas have access to programs requiring as little as 3-3.5% down. FHA loans require 3.5%, and VA loans (for veterans) require 0% down. Many Texas cities and counties also offer down payment assistance programs that can provide grants or low-interest loans to help cover your down payment. The key is shopping around with multiple lenders and exploring all available assistance programs.

What credit score do I need to buy a home in DFW? +

Most conventional loans require a credit score of at least 620. FHA loans may accept scores as low as 580 (or even 500 with 10% down). VA and USDA loans typically require 620 or higher. However, higher credit scores get better interest rates. A score of 740+ typically qualifies for the best rates, potentially saving you hundreds per month in interest. If your score is below 620, consider spending 6-12 months improving it before applying for a mortgage.

How long does it take to buy a home in Dallas-Fort Worth? +

From pre-approval to closing, expect 45-60 days in the DFW market. This includes house hunting time (which varies widely based on market conditions and your criteria), making an offer, the inspection period (typically 7-10 days in Texas), appraisal (1-2 weeks), and the closing process (usually 30-45 days after offer acceptance). In competitive markets, some buyers close in as little as 21 days, while others take several months to find the right home.

What are the best first-time home buyer programs in Texas? +

Texas offers several excellent programs: My First Texas Home provides down payment assistance up to 5% plus competitive rates. Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC) offers up to $15,000 in down payment grants. Local programs in Dallas, Fort Worth, Plano, and Frisco provide additional assistance. Federal programs include FHA loans (3.5% down), VA loans (0% down for veterans), and USDA loans (0% down for eligible rural areas). Work with a knowledgeable lender who can help you identify which programs you qualify for.

Should I buy or rent in DFW in 2026? +

If you plan to stay in the area for at least 3-5 years, have stable income, and can afford the down payment and monthly costs, buying often builds more wealth than renting in DFW's appreciating market. Rent in DFW continues rising (average $1,400-$2,000/month for a 2-bedroom), while a mortgage payment locks in your housing cost and builds equity. However, renting offers flexibility if you may relocate, don't have down payment savings, or prefer not to handle maintenance. Use a rent-vs-buy calculator with your specific numbers to make the best decision for your situation.
